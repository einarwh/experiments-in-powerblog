# I refuse to play the imitation game

<p class="blog-post-date">April 15, 2026</p>

There's a recurring theme whenever AI slopheads defend their use of generative tools for writing. They seem to think that it's some sort of gotcha that as a reader, I may not be able to distinguish perfectly between human-written and AI-generated text. HA! HA! Checkmate! But it's not. It's not checkmate, and it's not a gotcha.

They're trying to cast the situation as an instance of Turing's imitation game. Suddenly it's on me to prove that I can successfully identify human-written and AI-generated text 100% of the time. And if I can't, well, that must prove that there is no meaningful difference between the two. 

But here's the crux: I have no interest in playing that game. None. Why would I? 

AI slopheads are vastly overestimating my commitment to reading any particular piece of text, as if text were somehow scarce and hard to come by. But that's absurd. There is an unimaginable abundance of text. There's text everywhere. If I start reading something and it seems like slop to me, I stop reading. I move on. I read something else. 

But A-HA! GOTCHA! the slophead who put their name to the text might exclaim; I actually wrote that text! It wasn't generated! That's my vapid prose! To which I can only say WEIRD FLEX! Except I can't say that, because I'm not there, I'm gone (as the song goes). I have no interest in spending my limited time on Earth in fine-sorting bad human-written text from bad AI-generated text. There's an opportunity cost to playing the imitation game, and I'm not paying it. I simply turn my attention somewhere else as quickly as possible to minimize the waste. 

In the movie War Games, the super computer describes nuclear war as "a strange game", and notes that "the only winning move is not to play". It is the same with the imitation game. I refuse to play. And I win. 

AI slopheads seem to think that what I'm describing is impossible, because soon ALL TEXT WILL BE SHIT. That's not only obviously wrong, it also speaks volumes about the slopheads themselves.

HAVE YOU HEARD OF BOOKS? People have been writing them for a very long time! Do you think I'll run out of human-written books to read before I'm dead? There are literally hundreds of millions of books. In a good year, I read fifty. There are enough books in my local library to last me many lifetimes over, and I can assure you that I will read ALL OF THEM before I return to your text of Schrödinger's slop with a fine-grained comb. I wouldn't wait.

But maybe all the AI slopheads ever read these days is slop, and so they assume that it's the same for everyone else. It's not. We are not the same. Wherever they are, I'll be somewhere else, tapping into the vast reservoir of human-written texts, with no Turing game in sight. What should I read next? "Tools for Conviviality" by Ivan Illich? "Sculptor's Daughter" by Tove Jansson? "At Swim-Two-Birds" by Flann O'Brian? "Known and Strange Things" by Teju Cole? "The Moronic Inferno" by Martis Amis? "The Faraway Nearby" by Rebecca Solnit? "The Book of Disquiet" by Fernando Pessoa? Or maybe I should just drop by the library and see what catches my fancy?
